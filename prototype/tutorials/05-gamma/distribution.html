Choose the Distribution Family

Blood pressure is strictly positive and tends to be right-skewed (Mean = 131.7 > Median = 130). Which distribution is appropriate?

Gamma

$\text{Var}(Y) = \frac{\mu^2}{\text{shape}}$

Variance proportional to mean squared. Right-skewed, positive support.

Support: $(0, \infty)$

Gaussian (Normal)

$\text{Var}(Y) = \sigma^2$

Constant variance, symmetric. The "default" for continuous data.

Support: $(-\infty, \infty)$

Inverse Gaussian

$\text{Var}(Y) = \frac{\mu^3}{\lambda}$

Variance proportional to mean cubed. Heavier right tail than Gamma.

Support: $(0, \infty)$

Log-Normal

$\text{Var}(Y) = \mu^2(e^{\sigma^2}-1)$

Log-transform then Gaussian. Common alternative for positive data.

Support: $(0, \infty)$

✓ Gamma Distribution Selected

The Gamma distribution is ideal for positive continuous data with variance proportional to the mean squared.

✔ Correct! Gamma is ideal for positive continuous data.

The Gamma distribution is perfect for blood pressure because:

  • Positive support: Only produces values > 0
  • Right-skewed: Matches the asymmetry in our data
  • Variance ~ mean²: Higher BP â†’ more variability

(1) Positive predictions guaranteed

❌ Wrong! Gaussian allows negative predictions.

The Gaussian (Normal) distribution has support on $(-\infty, \infty)$, meaning it allows both positive and negative values.

Problem: Blood pressure can never be negative!

With Gaussian, extreme predictions could give negative BP values.

Other issues with Gaussian for blood pressure:

  • Constant variance: Assumes same variability at all BP levels
  • Symmetric: BP data is often right-skewed
  • Higher AIC: 2528.5 vs 2512.8 for Gamma (Gamma fits better!)

Gaussian is appropriate for truly continuous data that can be negative (e.g., temperature in Celsius, profit/loss). For strictly positive data, use Gamma or another positive-support distribution.

⚠ Valid alternative, but Gamma is simpler.

The Inverse Gaussian is another member of the exponential family that supports only positive values. It's a valid choice!

Variance: $\text{Var}(Y) = \mu^3/\lambda$

Variance increases even faster with the mean (cubic vs quadratic).

When to use Inverse Gaussian:

  • Data has an even heavier right tail than Gamma
  • Often used for waiting times and reliability data
  • More extreme variance-mean relationship needed

For blood pressure, the Gamma distribution typically provides a better fit because the variance-mean relationship is closer to quadratic than cubic. Try both and compare AIC if unsure!

⚠ Common alternative, but not a GLM.

The Log-Normal approach is very common for positive continuous data: transform Y to log(Y) and fit a Gaussian model.

Approach: $\log(Y) \sim N(\mu, \sigma^2)$

Coefficients still have multiplicative interpretation on the original scale.

Why Gamma GLM is often preferred:

  • Models E[Y] directly: Gamma models the mean; log-transform models E[log(Y)]
  • Proper GLM: Stays in the exponential family framework
  • Inference: Standard errors and tests are for E[Y], not geometric mean
  • Zeros: Gamma can't handle zeros, but neither can log-transform

Both are valid approaches! Log-Normal is simpler conceptually, while Gamma GLM is more principled for modelling the arithmetic mean.
