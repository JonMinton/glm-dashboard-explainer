Choose the Distribution Family

Exam proportions are continuous and bounded between 0 and 1. Which distribution is appropriate for modelling continuous proportions?

Beta

$\text{Var}(Y) = \frac{\mu(1-\mu)}{1 + \phi}$

Continuous on (0,1), flexible shape controlled by precision parameter $\phi$.

Support: $(0, 1)$

Gaussian (Normal)

$\text{Var}(Y) = \sigma^2$

Constant variance, symmetric. The "default" for continuous data.

Support: $(-\infty, \infty)$

Binomial

$\text{Var}(Y) = \frac{\mu(1-\mu)}{n}$

For counts of successes out of a known number of trials.

Support: $\{0/n, 1/n, \ldots, n/n\}$

Uniform

$\text{Var}(Y) = \frac{1}{12}$

All values equally likely. No relationship with predictors.

Support: $(0, 1)$

✓ Beta Distribution Selected

The Beta distribution is ideal for continuous proportions with flexible shape.

✔ Correct! Beta is the natural distribution for continuous proportions.

The Beta distribution is perfect for exam proportions because:

  • Support on (0,1): Only produces valid proportions
  • Flexible shape: Can be symmetric, left-skewed, or right-skewed
  • Precision parameter $\phi$: Controls how tightly data clusters around the mean

How Beta compares to alternatives with similar support or link:

Family Support Variance Link
Gaussian $(-\infty, \infty)$ $\sigma^2$ Identity
Binomial $\{0/n, \ldots, n/n\}$ $\mu(1-\mu)/n$ Logit
Beta $(0, 1)$ $\mu(1-\mu)/(1+\phi)$ Logit

Key insight: The Beta + logit combination gives us bounded predictions and a continuous distribution for the response. Think of it as "continuous logistic regression."

❌ Wrong! Gaussian is defined on the entire real line.

The Gaussian (Normal) distribution has support on $(-\infty, \infty)$, meaning predictions can exceed the $[0, 1]$ boundaries.

Problem: Proportions must stay between 0 and 1!

A Gaussian model could predict $\mu = 1.3$ or $\mu = -0.2$, both nonsensical for proportions.

Other issues:

  • Constant variance: Proportions near 0 or 1 naturally have less variability
  • Symmetric: Proportions near boundaries are typically skewed

For continuous data bounded between 0 and 1, the Beta distribution is the appropriate choice.

❌ Wrong! Binomial is for counts, not continuous proportions.

The Binomial distribution models the number of successes out of a known number of trials:

$Y \sim \text{Binomial}(n, p)$ gives counts: $Y \in \{0, 1, 2, \ldots, n\}$

Even as proportions ($Y/n$), the values are discrete: $\{0, 1/n, 2/n, \ldots, 1\}$

Problem: Our exam proportion (e.g., 0.73) is a continuous value, not a count divided by a known total. A value like 0.73 is not a valid count.

Binomial is for situations like "5 out of 10 patients recovered" where you know both the number of successes and the number of trials. For a continuous proportion, use the Beta distribution.

❌ Wrong! Uniform assumes all proportions are equally likely.

The Uniform distribution on $(0,1)$ assigns equal probability density to every value:

$f(y) = 1$ for all $y \in (0, 1)$

This means 0.1 is just as likely as 0.9 — regardless of any predictors!

Problems:

  • No parameters: Cannot model relationships with predictors
  • No flexibility: The distribution shape is always flat
  • Ignores data: Study hours, GPA, and tutoring would have no effect

We need a distribution that can change shape based on predictor values. The Beta distribution can be symmetric, left-skewed, or right-skewed depending on its parameters.
