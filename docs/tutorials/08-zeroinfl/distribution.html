Stage 3: The Problem Isn't Overdispersion — It's a Mixture

The data contains two types of zeros, generated by completely different processes:

Structural Zeros

People who would never visit regardless of their health needs. They are outside the "at risk" population.

Example: No insurance and clinic is 25km away — access barriers make visiting effectively impossible.

Sampling Zeros

People who could visit but happened to have zero visits this year. They are in the "at risk" population but drew a zero from the Poisson.

Example: Healthy 25-year-old with insurance — able to visit but didn't need to this year.

The ZIP Solution

The Zero-Inflated Poisson (ZIP) model explicitly separates these two processes:

  • Zero-inflation component (logistic): models the probability $\pi$ of being a structural zero
  • Count component (Poisson): models the expected count $\mu$ for the "at risk" population

The probability of observing $y = 0$ is:

$P(Y = 0) = \pi + (1 - \pi) \cdot e^{-\mu}$

The first term captures structural zeros, the second captures sampling zeros.

✓ Zero-Inflated Poisson (ZIP) Selected

ZIP explicitly models the two types of zeros: structural (from the inflation component) and sampling (from the Poisson component). This gives more accurate predictions and reveals which factors drive each process.
