The ZIP Distribution: A Mixture

The Zero-Inflated Poisson is built from two ingredients: a hidden Bernoulli "gate" that decides whether person $i$ is a structural zero (probability $\pi_i$), and a Poisson distribution with mean $\mu_i$ for those who pass the gate. Both quantities are driven by their own linear predictors:

$$\pi_i = \text{logit}^{-1}(z_i\gamma) = \frac{1}{1 + e^{-z_i\gamma}} \qquad\qquad \mu_i = \exp(x_i\beta)$$

Why $y = 0$ Has Two Routes

A zero can be produced by either branch of the mixture, so its probability is the sum of the two routes:

Route 1: Structural

The gate closes: person $i$ is a structural zero. The count is $0$ with certainty.

$P(\text{route 1}) = \pi_i$

Route 2: Sampling

The gate opens (probability $1-\pi_i$), but the Poisson happens to draw $0$.

$P(\text{route 2}) = (1-\pi_i)\, e^{-\mu_i}$

This gives the full probability mass function:

$$P(Y_i = y_i) = \begin{cases} \pi_i + (1-\pi_i)\, e^{-\mu_i} & \text{if } y_i = 0 \\[8pt] (1-\pi_i)\, \dfrac{e^{-\mu_i}\mu_i^{y_i}}{y_i!} & \text{if } y_i > 0 \end{cases}$$

Why $y > 0$ Has Only One Route

A positive count proves the gate was open — structural zeros can never produce $y = 3$. So the $y_i > 0$ case is simply the Poisson probability multiplied by the probability $(1-\pi_i)$ of being in the at-risk population at all. Observing $y_i > 0$ resolves the hidden label completely; observing $y_i = 0$ leaves it ambiguous, which is exactly why the E-step on the Fitting page only had to soft-assign the zeros.

Deriving the Log-Likelihood

As in every tutorial, the log-likelihood is the sum of the log-probabilities the model assigns to each observed data point. The only novelty is that the formula differs depending on whether $y_i$ is zero or positive.

Step 1: Take Logs of the PMF, Case by Case

For a zero observation, we must take the log of the sum of the two routes — it does not split further:

$$\ell_i = \ln\!\left[\pi_i + (1-\pi_i)\, e^{-\mu_i}\right] \qquad (y_i = 0)$$

For a positive observation, the log of the product separates into a sum:

$$\ell_i = \ln(1-\pi_i) + y_i \ln\mu_i - \mu_i - \ln(y_i!) \qquad (y_i > 0)$$
Step 2: Sum Over All Observations
$$\ell(\beta, \gamma) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \begin{cases} \ln\!\left[\pi_i + (1-\pi_i)\, e^{-\mu_i}\right] & \text{if } y_i = 0 \\[6pt] \ln(1-\pi_i) + y_i\ln\mu_i - \mu_i - \ln(y_i!) & \text{if } y_i > 0 \end{cases}$$
Step 3: Substitute the Linear Predictors

Writing $\pi_i = \text{logit}^{-1}(z_i\gamma)$ and $\mu_i = e^{x_i\beta}$ makes $\ell$ a function of the seven coefficients alone. Splitting the sum into the zero and positive observations:

$$\ell(\beta, \gamma) = \sum_{i:\, y_i = 0} \ln\!\left[\pi_i + (1-\pi_i)\, e^{-e^{x_i\beta}}\right] \;+\; \sum_{i:\, y_i > 0} \left[\ln(1-\pi_i) + y_i x_i\beta - e^{x_i\beta} - \ln(y_i!)\right]$$
The ZIP Log-Likelihood:

$$\ell(\beta, \gamma) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \begin{cases} \ln\!\left[\pi_i + (1-\pi_i)\, e^{-\mu_i}\right] & \text{if } y_i = 0 \\[6pt] \ln(1-\pi_i) + y_i\ln\mu_i - \mu_i - \ln(y_i!) & \text{if } y_i > 0 \end{cases}$$
$$\text{with } \pi_i = \text{logit}^{-1}(z_i\gamma), \qquad \mu_i = \exp(x_i\beta)$$

Reading the Two Cases

The two cases play different roles in estimation:

  • The $y_i = 0$ case couples $\gamma$ and $\beta$. The log-of-a-sum term $\ln[\pi_i + (1-\pi_i)e^{-\mu_i}]$ cannot be separated into a "$\gamma$ part" plus a "$\beta$ part" — both components compete to explain each zero. This term is why ZIP is not an exponential-family GLM and why IRLS alone cannot fit it.
  • The $y_i > 0$ case cleanly separates. The term $\ln(1-\pi_i)$ involves only $\gamma$, while $y_i\ln\mu_i - \mu_i - \ln(y_i!)$ is exactly the Poisson log-likelihood from Tutorial 3, involving only $\beta$.

Connection to the EM Algorithm

The soft weight from the Fitting page, $w_i = \pi_i / [\pi_i + (1-\pi_i)e^{-\mu_i}]$, is just Bayes' theorem applied to the two routes: the structural route's share of the total $P(Y_i = 0)$. The awkward log-of-a-sum is exactly the term EM tames — given the soft labels, the likelihood splits into a logistic piece for $\gamma$ and a Poisson piece for $\beta$, each fittable with the standard machinery.

Gradient Sketch

Direct optimisers like BFGS need the gradient. For the count coefficients, differentiating gives:

$$\frac{\partial \ell}{\partial \beta_j} = \sum_{i:\, y_i = 0} \frac{-(1-\pi_i)\, e^{-\mu_i}\,\mu_i}{\pi_i + (1-\pi_i)\, e^{-\mu_i}}\, x_{ij} \;+\; \sum_{i:\, y_i > 0} (y_i - \mu_i)\, x_{ij}$$

The positive-count part is the familiar Poisson score $\sum (y_i - \mu_i)x_{ij}$. The zero part is new: each zero pulls $\mu_i$ down, but the pull is scaled by $1 - w_i$, the probability the zero is a sampling zero. Zeros the model attributes to the structural route barely influence $\beta$ at all — the mixture automatically discounts them.

Log-Likelihood Comparison: The Count-Model Family

Tutorial Distribution Log-Likelihood (one observation, simplified) Handles
3 (Poisson) Poisson $y_i\ln\mu_i - \mu_i$ Counts, variance $=$ mean
4 (NegBin) Negative Binomial $\ln\Gamma(y_i+\theta) + y_i\ln\mu_i - (y_i+\theta)\ln(\theta+\mu_i) + \dots$ Overdispersion
8 (ZIP) Zero-Inflated Poisson $\ln[\pi_i + (1-\pi_i)e^{-\mu_i}]$ or $\ln(1-\pi_i) + y_i\ln\mu_i - \mu_i$ Excess zeros (mixture)

The Pattern Completed

NegBin generalised Poisson by letting the rate vary (a Poisson-Gamma mixture over a continuous range). ZIP generalises it differently: a two-point mixture where one component is degenerate at zero. The same recipe extends further — replace the Poisson branch with NegBin and you get ZINB, handling overdispersion and excess zeros at once.
